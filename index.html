Level 7 → 6

Grip Fundamentals

The grip is the only connection between you and the racquet. Getting it right from day one prevents bad habits that are hard to unlearn.

🤚

Continental Grip

Used for serve, volleys, and overhead. Hold the racquet as if shaking hands with it — bevel 2 for right-handers.

Serve · Volley · Overhead · Slice
✋

Eastern Forehand Grip

Flat on bevel 3. Great for Level 7 beginners — easier to achieve clean contact with a flat ball flight.

Flat Forehand · Level 7 Start
🖐

Semi-Western Forehand Grip

Palm slightly under the handle (bevel 4). Promotes topspin naturally — the goal for Level 6 players.

Topspin Forehand · Level 6
👋

Eastern Backhand Grip

Knuckle on bevel 1 (one-handed) or continental + eastern for two-handed. Required for topspin backhand.

One-handed Backhand · Two-handed
💡

Coach Tip: Use the "shake hands" cue for continental and the "gun to the floor" cue to rotate into semi-western. Never squeeze — hold it like a small bird.

Grip Drills

1
Edge Dribbling

Bounce the ball on the racquet edge (continental grip) 20 times. Builds feel and correct grip pressure.

2
Grip Switch Drill

Hold continental, tap ball up, switch to semi-western, tap again. Repeat for 30 seconds to build muscle memory.

3
Fence Swings

Practice your swing in front of a fence mirror. Check grip position before every swing — 50 reps per grip.

Level 7 → 6

Forehand Groundstroke

The forehand is usually the most natural shot. For Level 7, focus on contact point and follow-through. Level 6 adds topspin and cross-court direction.

1

Ready Position

Weight on toes, knees bent, racquet out front in continental. Eyes on the incoming ball.

2

Split Step

As opponent strikes, hop and land with feet shoulder-width apart. This primes explosive movement.

3

Unit Turn

Rotate shoulders and hips together as a unit. Bring the non-dominant hand and racquet back simultaneously.

4

Low-to-High Swing Path

Drop the racquet head below the ball (butt cap pointing down), then swing low-to-high through contact to generate topspin (Level 6).

5

Contact Point

Strike the ball in front of the front hip, at roughly waist height. Arm slightly bent at contact.

6

Follow-Through

Finish with the racquet high over the non-dominant shoulder ("windshield wiper" for topspin) or across the body for flat.

⚠ Common Mistakes

  • Late contact: Ball gets behind the hip. Fix — earlier unit turn.
  • Arm only: No hip/shoulder rotation. Fix — practice shadow swings leading with the hip.
  • Stiff arm: Locking the elbow. Fix — keep a slight bend throughout the swing.
  • Wrong grip at contact: Grip slips during swing. Fix — check grip pressure (firm but not white-knuckle).

Forehand Drills

1
Shadow Swings (L7)

Without a ball, do 50 forehand swings focusing on unit turn → low-to-high swing path → follow-through.

2
Hand-Feed Rally (L7)

Partner hand-feeds 20 balls to your forehand side. Focus on contact point in front of the body only.

3
Deep Cross-Court (L6)

Rally cross-court forehands targeting the service box extension. 3 sets × 10 consecutive.

4
Cone Target Drill (L6)

Place cones at corners. Hit 10 forehands at each cone. Track accuracy percentage.

5
Topspin Wall Rally (L6)

Hit forehands against a practice wall with a low-to-high swing, aiming above a 1-meter tape line for 2 minutes.

Level 7 → 6

Backhand Groundstroke

Level 7 players learn the two-handed backhand for stability. Level 6 players refine topspin and begin exploring the slice backhand.

1

Two-Handed Grip Setup

Dominant hand in eastern backhand grip (bevel 1), non-dominant hand above in eastern forehand grip. Both hands work together.

2

Early Preparation

As soon as ball crosses the net, rotate away from the net — both hands on the racquet, weight shifting to the back foot.

3

Hip Drive

Drive the front hip toward the target to generate power. The non-dominant arm pulls through as the lead force.

4

Contact Point

Ball struck in front of the body (further in front than forehand) at waist height. Racquet face slightly closed at contact for topspin.

5

Follow-Through

Finish high — racquet ends up over the front shoulder, both arms extended. Hips fully rotated to face the net.

🍰 Slice Backhand (Level 6+)

Switch to continental grip. High-to-low swing path — racquet brushes under the ball. Great for defensive shots, approach shots, and breaking up opponents' rhythm. Keep the follow-through low and long, not chopping down.

⚠ Common Mistakes

  • Pulling the left hand off too early: Causes the ball to go wide. Keep both hands on until after contact.
  • Too much arm: Not using the hip/shoulder. Fix — shadow swings leading with the hip.
  • Too late: Contacting the ball beside or behind the body. Fix — turn earlier.

Backhand Drills

1
Wall Tap Drill (L7)

Stand 2m from a wall. Tap the ball softly with two-handed backhand strokes for 1 minute. Focus on keeping both hands connected.

2
Feed & Rally (L7)

Partner feeds 20 balls to the backhand side. Focus only on hitting in front of the body.

3
Down-the-Line Backhand (L6)

Rally backhand down the line. 3 sets × 8 consecutive. Build directional control.

4
Slice & Approach (L6)

Feed a short ball; hit a slice backhand approach, then close to the net for a volley.

Level 7 → 6

The Serve

Level 7 begins with the underhand/push serve or beginner overhand. Level 6 develops a full continental-grip flat serve with consistent ball toss.

1

Stance & Grip

Stand sideways at the baseline (platform or pinpoint stance). Use continental grip — critical for long-term development even at Level 7.

2

Ball Toss

Hold the ball with fingertips, not the palm. Toss to 1 o'clock (slightly right and in front), roughly 12–18 inches above contact. This is the #1 skill to practice separately.

3

Trophy Position

As the ball rises, bend the knees and load weight on the back leg. Racquet arm forms an L-shape (trophy position). Both eyes on the ball.

4

Leg Drive

Extend the knees to drive upward as you swing. The serve is a full-body shot, not just an arm swing.

5

Contact & Pronation

Strike the ball at full arm extension. Pronate the forearm (turn like tightening a screwdriver) at contact for pace and spin control.

6

Follow-Through

Finish with the racquet arm crossing to the opposite hip. Land inside the baseline on the front foot.

💡

Ball Toss Practice: Toss the ball and let it drop — it should land inside your front foot, slightly to the right. Do 20 tosses without swinging to master this first.

⚠ Common Mistakes

  • Wrong grip: Eastern forehand grip on serve limits pronation and ceiling height.
  • Inconsistent toss: The single biggest cause of double faults. Fix — isolate toss practice daily.
  • No leg drive: Serving only with the arm. Fix — practice "trophy jump" drills.
  • Elbow drop: Dropping the elbow in the backswing creates a sling motion. Fix — keep the elbow high (L-shape).

Serve Drills

1
Isolated Ball Toss (L7+)

Toss the ball 50 times without swinging. It must land consistently on a coin placed on the court.

2
Short Court Serve (L7)

Serve from inside the service line. Reduces distance, builds form confidence without power pressure.

3
Trophy Freeze (L7)

Practice swinging to the trophy position and holding it for 3 seconds. Check: back knee bent, elbow at ear level, eyes on ball spot.

4
Kick Serve Intro (L6)

Toss slightly to the left (11 o'clock). Swing upward and to the right with pronation. Aim for net clearance and heavy topspin.

5
Serve to Box (L6)

Target wide, body, and T with 10 serves each. Track percentage in each zone. Goal: 60%+ first serve in.

Level 6 Focus

Volley

Volleys are punch shots hit before the ball bounces. Beginners often over-swing — the key is a compact, firm-wristed block with continental grip.

1

Continental Grip (Always)

Both forehand and backhand volleys use the continental grip. No grip switch needed — makes volleying faster and more reliable.

2

Ready Position at Net

Stand 2–3 feet inside the service line. Knees bent, weight on toes, racquet out front at chest height.

3

Split Step

As your opponent strikes, split step to load your legs and be ready to move in any direction.

4

Punch & Block

Step forward (front foot toward the target) as you make contact. The swing is a compact punch — no backswing. Firm wrist throughout.

5

Angle & Placement

Angle the face slightly open (upward) for low balls, flatter for high balls above the net. Direct the ball cross-court or into open space.

🎯 Approach Shot → First Volley (Level 6)

When a short ball sits up, attack it with a deep approach shot (backhand slice or forehand drive), then immediately move forward. Split step as your opponent hits and punch your first volley for a winner.

⚠ Common Mistakes

  • Too much backswing: Causes late contact and errors. Fix — keep the racquet in front, punch only.
  • Wrong grip: Switching to forehand grip. Fix — drill with continental only.
  • Standing still: Not stepping through. Fix — practice "step and punch" footwork pattern.
  • Too far from net: Volleying from no-man's land. Fix — move up to just inside the service line.

Volley Drills

1
Catch & Release (L6)

Partner stands at baseline and gently tosses balls at the net player who blocks them back — no backswing allowed. 20 reps each side.

2
Two-Volley Combo (L6)

Baseline player feeds a ball; net player volleys, moves to close, and hits a second punch volley winner. 15 reps.

3
Rapid-Fire Volleys (L6)

Coach or partner feeds 5 quick balls alternating forehand and backhand volleys. No reset between shots. 4 sets.

4
Approach & Volley (L6)

Feed a short ball. Player hits approach shot, closes to net, and finishes with a volley. Run 20 complete sequences.

Level 6 Focus

Return of Serve

The return is the second most important shot in tennis. Level 6 players learn to neutralize the serve consistently and begin to apply direction.

1

Starting Position

Stand near the baseline corner, 1–2 feet behind the baseline (adjust for serve speed). Weight on toes, knees bent.

2

Read the Toss

Watch the server's ball toss to predict serve direction (wide, body, T). A toss to the right hints wide; centered hints T or body.

3

Split Step at Ball Strike

Hop and land right as the server's racquet makes contact with the ball. This is critical — timing the split step to the server, not the ball.

4

Compact Backswing

Use a shorter backswing than a groundstroke — the serve is already coming with pace. Unit turn is still essential.

5

Block or Drive

Level 6: Against fast serves, use a blocking motion (short swing). Against slower serves, drive through the ball with a regular groundstroke.

6

Direction & Recovery

Aim cross-court (safer target, larger court). After the return, immediately recover to the center baseline.

💡

First Priority: Get the ball back in play. Consistency beats aggression on returns at Levels 6–7. A return that lands in is always better than an error.

⚠ Common Mistakes

  • Too big a backswing: Ball arrives before the swing completes. Fix — shorten to a compact unit turn.
  • No split step: Caught flat-footed. Fix — rehearse the timing sequence daily.
  • Trying for a winner: Overaggressive error. Fix — aim cross-court, 3 feet over the net.

Return Drills

1
Serve & Return Rally (L6)

One player serves, the other returns cross-court — keep it going for 5+ shots. Switch roles every 10 serves.

2
Read the Toss (L6)

Server tosses the ball only — returner calls "wide", "T", or "body" before the toss reaches its peak. 20 reps to build anticipation.

3
Block Return (L6)

Slow-pitch machine or partner serves at 70% pace. Returner blocks the ball back with minimal swing — focus on solid contact only.

4
Return Down the Line (L6)

Partner serves wide. Returner redirects the ball down the line — advanced direction control drill.

Footwork & Movement

Great strokes fail without great footwork. These patterns apply at every level.

⬛

Split Step

A small hop landing on both feet as your opponent makes contact. Loads the legs for instant reaction in any direction. Do this on every single shot.

L7 & L6
↔️

Shuffle Steps

Side-to-side movement keeping you balanced and ready. Never cross your feet laterally when close to the ball.

L7 & L6
↗️

Crossover Step

For balls far to one side, lead with the outside foot crossing over to cover ground quickly before transitioning to shuffle steps.

L7 & L6
🔃

Recovery Step

After every shot, push off and recover to the center baseline (or net if at net position). Court coverage requires constant recovery.

L7 & L6
⬆️

Approach Step

When a short ball sits up, move forward using short adjustment steps to set up an ideal contact point, then attack through the ball.

L6
🎯

Open vs Closed Stance

Closed stance (stepping in with front foot) provides stability for beginners. Open stance (squaring to the net) allows faster recovery — Level 6 target.

Mental Game

Tennis is as much mental as physical. These principles apply from your very first match.

01

One Point at a Time

Each point is independent. A double fault doesn't cause the next one — reset your routine and start fresh every rally.

02

The Between-Point Routine

Use a 20-second reset: turn away from the court, take a deep breath, positive self-talk, then walk purposefully to the line.

03

Focus on Process, Not Score

Think about your technique cue ("low-to-high", "split step"), not the score. Execution focus reduces anxiety and improves results.

04

Positive Body Language

Walk tall, shoulders back, head up — even after errors. Opponent and crowd read body language. Project confidence to feel it.

05

Play to Your Strengths

At Levels 6–7, build patterns that lead to your best shots. Set up forehands. Avoid your weaker zones under pressure.

06

Embrace Errors

Errors are data, not failures. Every missed shot tells you something about contact point, swing path, or timing. Use them.
